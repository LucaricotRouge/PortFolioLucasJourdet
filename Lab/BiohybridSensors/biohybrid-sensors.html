Biohybrid Sensors

Project Overview

Based on the work of Nicolae et al."Biohybrid Devices" I replicate the "grow arround method" and "layering" to embed sensing capabilities in bacterial cellullose. In the course of this project I built a total of 3 different sensors, two of which were resistive (temperature and stretch sensors) and one capacitive (mutual capacitance matrix). Using the Arduino and other electronic components, I was able to design functional bio-hybrid sensors.

Temperature Sensor

How to create the temperature sensor :

To make this sensor I used a layering technique as presented in the paper. A thin copper wire was inserted into a scoby cut in half to give the sensor its resistive properties. To dry the bacterial cellulose, the scoby/copper wire assembly was placed in the oven for a few hours.

Calibration :

To make the sensor functional I used the simple principle of TCR (temperature coefficient of resistance) applied to copper wire and measured its slight variations in resistance due to a change in temperature. To do this I combined the temperature sensor with a Wheatstone bridge and an HX711, which I will calibrate to better display the temperature in the Arduino's serial monitor. The HX711 is mainly used to amplify the voltage variation detected at the terminal of the Wheatstone bridge. The calibration of the HX711 was done through experimental measurements at controlled temperature. To measure and display correct temperatures on the Arduino's serial port, I determined a temperature equation as a function of the voltage at the terminal of the Wheatstone bridge, which I then implemented in the Arduino code.

Stretch Sensor

How to create the stretch sensor :

To make this sensor I used the grow around method as presented in the paper. A thin conductive thread was placed in a mold and the scoby was allowed to grow around it for several days. Once the growth was complete, the sensor was removed from the mold and dried in an oven for a few hours.

Calibration :

To make the sensor functional I used the principle of piezoresistivity, which consists in measuring the variation of resistance of the sensor when it is stretched. To do this I combined the stretch sensor with a Wheatstone bridge and an HX711, which I calibrated to better display the elongation in the Arduino's serial monitor. The calibration of the HX711 was done through experimental measurements at controlled elongations. To measure and display correct elongations on the Arduino's serial port, I determined an elongation equation as a function of the voltage at the terminal of the Wheatstone bridge, which I then implemented in the Arduino code.

Mutual Capacitance Sensor

How to create the mutual capacitance sensor :

For this sensor I used an Arduino, a Muca sensor and the mutual capacitance matrix. To make it, I've used conductive yarn, a layering support and a scoby. The layering technique was used to make this sensor. The drying process is the same as for the other sensors. The soldering part is a bit tricky and we first had to remove some of the wire's protective insulation at the end with a soldering iron, put some extinguishant on it and solder it with a bad pin before reinforcing the whole thing with a sheath. As far as the Muca board is concerned, I downloaded the Muca library from GitHub and used the relevant functions to retrieve the useful data (getTouch() in particular, which provides the touch coordinates and weight). To make the visualisation of the touch more intuitive and visible, I created a display in Processing by treating the data returned on the Arduino's serial port. Unfortunately, the sensor contains a faulty row and column that distort the display on these axes (axes in red).
